Selected Projects

Feature Engineering & AutoML Evaluations RA: Columbia University

Python (scikit-learn, TensorFlow), cross-validation, feature engineering, reproducible workflows

Basic description: Developed and benchmarked machine learning pipelines for messy tabular datasets. I designed leakage-safe preprocessing (iterative/KNN imputers, standardized encodings, interaction terms) and compared curated pipelines against AutoML defaults across 30+ datasets. I tuned classical ML models and deep learning baselines with nested cross-validation, reporting AUROC gains of 2–6pp. I also produced reproducible Jupyter templates for policy and health datasets.

Abstract: This project systematically evaluated the role of feature engineering and pipeline design in generalization stability. By inducing MAR/MNAR missingness across diverse datasets, we demonstrated that curated pipelines reduce variance, improve AUROC performance, and outperform AutoML defaults. I explicitly linked algorithmic design choices—such as loss/activation functions and imputation strategies— to predictive outcomes. These findings show that reproducible, curated workflows enable more reliable deployment in irregular datasets common to economics, policy, and health research.

Environmental Determinants of Health (2000–2023) RA: Lafayette College

Data harmonization (EPA, NOAA, CDC WONDER), R/Python automation, mapping workflows, regression analysis

Basic description: Constructed a harmonized county-level dataset (2000–2023) linking mortality outcomes to environmental exposures (PM2.5, ozone, temperature, humidity, pressure). I automated EPA/NOAA/CDC data retrieval, standardized FIPS codes, resolved missing/suppressed values, and created reproducible R pipelines to map annual mortality rates (2013–2023). I also built composite air quality and weather indexes for long-term comparability.

Abstract: This project develops a harmonized 2000–2023 panel across 3,000+ counties, integrating environmental exposures and mortality outcomes. Using automated retrieval and reproducible pipelines, we linked EPA, NOAA, and CDC WONDER data while addressing missingness and boundary inconsistencies. Regression results show strong associations between PM2.5 exposure, colder temperatures, and elevated cardiovascular mortality—patterns concentrated in rural and southeastern U.S. counties. The dataset supports future quasi-experimental designs and provides policymakers with scalable tools for analyzing regional disparities in environmental health risks.

Decision Trees vs. Random Forests: Robustness on Noisy Tabular Data ML Benchmark

Model stability, hyperparameter sweeps, bootstrapped CIs, ablation

Basic description: Ran controlled experiments comparing single decision trees against Random Forests under conditions of label noise, class imbalance, and correlated features. Using Python (scikit-learn), I tested multiple hyperparameter settings, tracked performance across cross-validation folds, and summarized results with bootstrapped confidence intervals.

Abstract: Across synthetic and real-world tabular datasets, Random Forests consistently outperformed individual trees in both accuracy and variance stability. Under 10–30% symmetric label noise, RF retained accuracy within 2–5pp of clean baselines, while single trees degraded by 8–15pp. Hyperparameter sweeps showed shallow depth and sufficient estimators minimized variance without sacrificing bias. Feature correlation ablations further confirmed that RF’s ensemble decorrelation mitigates overfitting. My contributions included reproducible experimental code, systematic benchmarking, and clear result visualizations highlighting robustness–accuracy tradeoffs.

Urban Heat Island: Mapping & Microclimate Analysis Course Project

R (sf, osmdata, dplyr, ggplot2, lubridate), API ingestion (Open-Meteo / OWM), geospatial buffers, day–night temperature diagnostics

Basic description: Built a reproducible R workflow to study urban heat islands by (1) querying OpenStreetMap buildings within buffered areas, (2) fetching or simulating hourly weather histories, and (3) summarizing day vs. night temperatures at monthly cadence. I automated OSM pulls with osmdata, generated buffers via sf, and produced city-level comparisons (Chicago vs. Berlin) using dplyr/lubridate pipelines and ggplot2 visualizations.

Abstract: I combined geospatial footprints and high-frequency meteorology to quantify within-city microclimate differences that drive the urban heat island effect. Building footprints (OSM) inside a 500-m buffer provided a structural proxy for surface cover, while archived hourly temperatures (Open-Meteo; optional OWM function) were aggregated into day/night periods using sunrise/sunset logic. The pipeline exposes consistent nocturnal warming in dense cores and enables scalable comparisons across cities and seasons—supporting future integration with land-use, canopy cover, and vulnerability layers for policy analysis.

Election Campaign Projections Using Python CM151 Final Project

Python (pandas, matplotlib, seaborn), data cleaning, heatmap visualization, swing-state prediction

Basic description: Built a Python workflow to analyze historical U.S. election data, clean raw state-level results, and identify likely swing states for upcoming cycles. I automated data preparation by removing irrelevant variables, standardizing state identifiers, and computing margins of victory. Visualizations included heatmaps and bar charts of state competitiveness. Our analysis highlighted the battleground states most likely to shape the 2024 election.

Abstract: This project leverages federal election datasets (USA.gov, FEC) to evaluate historical voting patterns and predict pivotal battleground states. After cleaning and merging state-level results from the 2020 presidential election, we computed margins of victory and generated heatmaps to highlight states with razor-thin outcomes (GA, AZ, WI, PA, MI, NC, NV). Campaign visit data validated our predictions, showing that candidates focused heavily on the identified swing states. The model demonstrates how a data-driven approach can guide campaign resource allocation by forecasting which states are most decisive in close elections. My contributions included dataset integration, Python coding for cleaning and visualization, and interpreting results for strategic insights.
